In manufacturing company audits, I routinely review four numbers that most business owners track in isolation — if they track them at all. Salary structures, employee turnover, EBITDA margin, and current ratio are typically treated as separate concerns: HR deals with compensation, operations deals with turnover, finance tracks EBITDA, and accounting monitors the balance sheet.

But in every business I have audited that was underperforming, these four metrics were connected in a chain. I reviewed a mid-sized manufacturing company where turnover was running at 28% — significantly above the industry average of 19%. When I traced the cause, it led back to salaries that were 12–15% below market rate for key production roles. The company was "saving" on wages but spending far more on replacement costs — recruiting fees, training time, lost productivity during onboarding — that were being absorbed quietly into operating expenses. Their EBITDA margin was 7.2% when it should have been 11–12% for their sector. And their current ratio had drifted to 1.3 because the cash drain from constant rehiring was tightening their working capital.

Fix the salary structure → turnover drops → EBITDA recovers → current ratio stabilizes. These four metrics are a chain, not four separate problems. This guide and the four calculators below are built around that insight.

## Hourly to Salary Calculator

**The Problem:** You are hiring a contractor at $35/hour and considering converting them to a salaried employee. What annual salary offer is fair? Or you are a freelancer wondering if your $28/hour rate is equivalent to your friend's $58,000 salaried job. Without the right conversion, you are either overpaying or losing talent to competitors who did the math.

### The Hourly to Annual Salary Formula:

```
Annual Salary = Hourly Rate × Hours Per Week × Weeks Per Year
Standard full-time: Annual Salary = Hourly Rate × 40 × 52 = Hourly Rate × 2,080
```

### Simple Conversion Example:

**Scenario:** Your contractor charges $35/hour. What annual salary is equivalent?

```
Annual Salary = $35 × 2,080 = $72,800
```

But that is just the starting point. A true salary equivalent must account for benefits:

**True Cost Comparison:**

| Component | Hourly Worker | Salaried Employee |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| Base compensation | $35/hr ($72,800/yr) | $72,800/yr |
| Health insurance | Not provided | $7,000–$15,000/yr |
| Paid time off (15 days) | Not paid | $4,200/yr |
| Employer payroll taxes | ~7.65% | ~7.65% |
| 401(k) match (3%) | Not provided | $2,184/yr |
| **True total cost** | **$78,373** | **$97,534** |

💡 **Key Insight:** The contractor at $35/hour is actually cheaper than a $72,800 salaried employee when you factor in benefits. If you want true cost equivalency for a salaried hire, the hourly rate equivalent is closer to **$46.89/hour** — not $35. Missing this calculation leads to consistently underestimating true labor costs by $15,000–$30,000 per employee annually.

### Salary to Hourly Conversion (Reverse):

```
Hourly Rate = Annual Salary ÷ 2,080
```

**Example:** A $85,000/year salaried employee costs:
```
$85,000 ÷ 2,080 = $40.87/hour
```

This is critical for project costing — if you bill a client $75/hour for a $40.87/hour employee, your gross labor margin is **45.5%**.

## Employee Turnover Rate Calculator

**The Most Expensive Line Item Nobody Tracks:** Most businesses track revenue, COGS, and rent to the dollar — but have no idea what they spend annually on replacing people who leave. In 2026, the average US business spends **$42,000 replacing a mid-level employee** earning $60,000. That is 70% of annual salary, gone — in recruiting fees, lost productivity, training time, and onboarding costs.

### The Employee Turnover Rate Formula:

```
Turnover Rate (%) = (Employees Who Left ÷ Average Number of Employees) × 100
Average Employees = (Employees at Start of Period + Employees at End of Period) ÷ 2
```

### Turnover Rate Calculation Example:

**Scenario:** Your company starts the year with 85 employees, ends with 90, and 12 employees left during the year.

```
Average Employees = (85 + 90) ÷ 2 = 87.5
Turnover Rate = (12 ÷ 87.5) × 100 = 13.7%
```

At 13.7%, you are replacing 13.7% of your workforce annually — close to the US national average of 12–15%, but still costly.

📋 **Auditor's Note:**
In a manufacturing company audit I conducted, management was satisfied that turnover appeared "normal" at around 20%. When I broke down the turnover cost properly — recruiting fees, onboarding, training time, productivity loss during ramp-up, and overtime for remaining staff — the true annual replacement bill came to approximately $1.1 million for a 150-person operation. That figure had never been calculated before. It was being absorbed across multiple cost centers without anyone seeing the total. Once management saw the consolidated number, addressing retention became the highest-priority cost reduction initiative — ahead of material sourcing and energy costs.

## EBITDA Margin Calculator

**The Problem with Net Profit:** Net profit is influenced by financing decisions, tax strategies, and accounting methods — making it nearly impossible to compare two businesses fairly. EBITDA strips all of that away, giving you a clean view of operational profitability that is directly comparable across companies, industries, and time periods.

### The EBITDA Formula:

```
EBITDA = Net Income + Interest + Taxes + Depreciation + Amortization
```

Or calculated from revenue:

```
EBITDA = Revenue − Operating Expenses (excluding D&A, interest, taxes)
```

### The EBITDA Margin Formula:

```
EBITDA Margin (%) = (EBITDA ÷ Total Revenue) × 100
```

### EBITDA Margin Calculation Example:

**Scenario:** A professional services firm:

| Line Item | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Total Revenue | $2,400,000 |
| Cost of Goods Sold | $960,000 |
| Operating Expenses | $720,000 |
| Interest Expense | $85,000 |
| Taxes | $168,000 |
| Net Income | $467,000 |
| Depreciation & Amortization | $142,000 |

**EBITDA Calculation:**
```
EBITDA = $467,000 + $85,000 + $168,000 + $142,000 = $862,000
EBITDA Margin = ($862,000 ÷ $2,400,000) × 100 = 35.9%
```

At 35.9%, this is an **excellent** EBITDA margin for a professional services firm.

### Why EBITDA Matters Beyond Internal Analysis:

**Business Valuation:**
Most business acquisitions are priced as a multiple of EBITDA:

| Business Type | Typical EBITDA Multiple |
|--------------|------------------------|
| Small business (<$1M EBITDA) | 3–5× |
| Mid-market ($1M–$10M EBITDA) | 5–8× |
| Large/established (>$10M) | 8–15× |
| High-growth SaaS | 15–25× |

💰 **Quick Win:** Improving EBITDA by 20% on a business valued at 5× EBITDA adds 20% to your exit value — without selling a single extra unit. A $500,000 EBITDA business is worth $2.5M; a $600,000 EBITDA business is worth $3.0M. Operational efficiency is the fastest path to business value creation.

**Loan Qualification (DSCR):**
Banks use EBITDA to calculate Debt Service Coverage Ratio:
```
DSCR = EBITDA ÷ Annual Debt Service (Principal + Interest)
```
Most lenders require DSCR > 1.25. Below 1.0 means your EBITDA cannot cover debt payments — loan denied.

## Current Ratio Analysis Calculator

**The Question Every Lender Asks First:** "Can this business pay its bills for the next 12 months without emergency financing?" Your Current Ratio answers it in a single number.

### The Current Ratio Formula:

```
Current Ratio = Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities
```

**Where:**
- **Current Assets:** Cash, accounts receivable, inventory, prepaid expenses — anything convertible to cash within 12 months
- **Current Liabilities:** Accounts payable, short-term debt, accrued expenses, current portion of long-term debt — anything due within 12 months

### Current Ratio Calculation Example:

**Scenario:** A manufacturing business preparing for a bank loan application:

**Current Assets:**
| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Cash & equivalents | $85,000 |
| Accounts receivable | $142,000 |
| Inventory | $98,000 |
| Prepaid expenses | $18,000 |
| **Total Current Assets** | **$343,000** |

**Current Liabilities:**
| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Accounts payable | $72,000 |
| Accrued expenses | $38,000 |
| Short-term loan | $45,000 |
| Current portion of long-term debt | $28,000 |
| **Total Current Liabilities** | **$183,000** |

```
Current Ratio = $343,000 ÷ $183,000 = 1.87
```

A ratio of 1.87 is **healthy** — above the 1.5 lender minimum and approaching the 2.0 ideal.

### How to Interpret Your Current Ratio:

| Current Ratio | Status | Lender View |
|--------------|--------|-------------|
| Below 1.0 | 🔴 Critical | Loan almost certainly denied |
| 1.0–1.2 | 🟠 Concerning | High scrutiny, higher rates |
| 1.2–1.5 | 🟡 Workable | Loan possible with strong other metrics |
| 1.5–2.0 | 🟢 Healthy | Standard loan approval range |
| 2.0–3.0 | 🟢 Strong | Strong approval, favorable terms |
| Above 3.0 | 🔵 Review Needed | May be holding excess idle cash |

⚠️ **Critical Mistake:** Checking current ratio only at year-end and ignoring it the other 11 months. A ratio of 2.1 in January can become 0.9 in April if seasonal cash outflows are not planned for. Monitor monthly — especially in businesses with seasonal revenue patterns.

## How All 4 Metrics Connect: The Business Health Chain

```
Salary Decisions → Turnover Rate → EBITDA Margin → Current Ratio
```

**Here is how the chain works in practice:**

**Step 1 — Underpriced Salaries:**
You hire at $18/hour ($37,440/year) when the market rate is $22/hour ($45,760/year).

**Step 2 — High Turnover:**
Underpaid employees leave at 35% turnover rate vs industry average of 15%. You are replacing 20% more of your workforce than competitors.

**Step 3 — Compressed EBITDA:**
Each replacement costs $25,000. In a 50-person company with 35% turnover = 17.5 replacements × $25,000 = **$437,500 in turnover costs annually** — hitting your operating expenses and crushing EBITDA margin.

**Step 4 — Weak Current Ratio:**
High operating costs from turnover drain cash. Current liabilities rise. Current ratio falls below 1.5. Bank denies the expansion loan.

**The Fix Runs Backwards:**
Pay $22/hour (correct salary) → Turnover drops to 12% → Turnover costs drop by $250,000 → EBITDA margin rises 3–4 points → Cash position improves → Current ratio returns to 2.0+ → Loan approved.

**The paradox:** Paying employees more often costs the business less. The businesses that underpay to "save money" are often the ones spending the most on recruitment.

## 7 Common Mistakes That Distort Your HR & Financial Metrics

### 1. ❌ Using Gross Hourly Rate Without Benefits Loading

**The Mistake:** Comparing contractor rate ($40/hr) to employee salary ($83,200/yr = $40/hr) as if they are equal.

**The Fix:** Add 25–35% employer burden to any salaried employee cost before comparing to contractor rates. Missing this underestimates true labor costs by $15,000–$30,000 per employee annually.

### 2. ❌ Counting Only Voluntary Turnover

**The Mistake:** Only tracking resignations, not including involuntary terminations in your turnover rate.

**The Fix:** Track total separations — voluntary and involuntary. High involuntary turnover signals a hiring quality problem. High voluntary turnover signals a retention problem. You need to know which is which.

### 3. ❌ Using Net Income Instead of EBITDA for Comparison

**The Mistake:** Comparing your 8% net margin to a competitor's 12% net margin and assuming they are more profitable at the operational level.

**The Fix:** Compare EBITDA margins. Your competitor might have lower debt interest or different depreciation schedules — neither reflects true operational performance.

### 4. ❌ Checking Current Ratio Only at Year-End

**The Mistake:** Looking at current ratio only during annual accounting.

**The Fix:** Calculate monthly. A ratio of 2.1 in January can become 0.9 in April if seasonal cash outflows are not planned for.

### 5. ❌ Ignoring Industry-Specific Turnover Benchmarks

**The Mistake:** Panicking about 25% turnover in hospitality (below average) or feeling comfortable about 25% turnover in tech (dangerously high).

**The Fix:** Always compare turnover against your industry benchmark, not the national average.

### 6. ❌ Treating EBITDA as Cash Flow

**The Mistake:** Spending based on EBITDA thinking it equals available cash.

**The Fix:** EBITDA does not account for capital expenditures, working capital changes, or debt principal repayments. True free cash ≈ EBITDA - Capex - Debt Principal - Working Capital Changes.

### 7. ❌ Optimizing Current Ratio at the Expense of Profitability

**The Mistake:** Holding excess cash to boost current ratio while that cash could be deployed in revenue-generating investments.

**The Fix:** A current ratio above 3.0 often signals under-investment. Target 1.5–2.5. Redeploy the excess into the business.

## 5 Proven Strategies to Improve All 4 Metrics

### Strategy 1: Annual Compensation Benchmarking

Every January, run every salaried position through the **[Hourly to Salary Calculator](/calculator/hourly-to-salary)** and compare against market data.

**The Rule:** Any position more than 10% below market rate gets a salary adjustment plan within 90 days.

**The Math:** If a $55,000/year employee is 15% below market ($63,250), giving a $8,250 raise seems expensive. But if it prevents a $40,000 replacement cost, you have **saved $31,750 net** — plus retained institutional knowledge.

**Expected Outcomes:**
- Turnover rate: Drops 8–15 percentage points within 12 months
- EBITDA margin: Improves 2–4 points as replacement costs drop
- Current ratio: Improves 0.2–0.4 as operating cash stabilizes

### Strategy 2: Turnover Root Cause Analysis

For every voluntary separation, conduct a structured exit interview covering compensation, management quality, growth opportunities, and work-life balance.

**Track quarterly:** Turnover by department, manager, and tenure band (0–6 months, 6–12 months, 1–3 years, 3+ years).

- **High early turnover (0–6 months):** Hiring or onboarding problem
- **High mid-tenure (1–3 years):** Career growth or compensation problem
- **High senior turnover (3+ years):** Management or culture problem

**Expected EBITDA Impact:** Reducing turnover from 20% to 12% in a 100-person, $60,000 average salary business saves approximately **$336,000 annually** — directly adding to EBITDA.

### Strategy 3: Monthly EBITDA Review with Variance Analysis

Build a simple monthly P&L that calculates EBITDA margin automatically. Compare to prior month and prior year same month. Investigate any margin compression greater than 1.5 points.

**Early warning system:** A business that checks EBITDA monthly catches margin erosion at -2 points. A business that checks annually finds out at -8 points — when it is much harder to reverse.

> **Track Monthly:** Use our **[EBITDA Margin Calculator](/calculator/ebitda-margin-calc)** every month-end.

### Strategy 4: 13-Week Cash Flow Forecast

Build a rolling 13-week cash flow projection. Each week, update actuals and reforecast the next 13 weeks.

**Key metrics to track weekly:**
- Days Sales Outstanding (DSO): Target < 35 days
- Days Payable Outstanding (DPO): Target 30–45 days
- Cash Conversion Cycle: DSO + Inventory Days − DPO (lower is better)

**Current Ratio Goal:** Keep a minimum of 1.6 as your internal floor. If approaching 1.6, trigger a cash generation action plan before it hits 1.5.

### Strategy 5: Connect Compensation to Business Performance

Implement a structured compensation framework:

| Tier | Criteria | Salary Band | Increase Trigger |
|------|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Entry | Learning, supervised | Market −10% to Market | Skill milestones |
| Proficient | Independent, reliable | Market rate | Annual performance |
| Senior | Mentors others | Market +10–15% | Business impact |
| Expert | Critical/irreplaceable | Market +20–30% | Retention + performance |

**Why it improves all 4 metrics:** Salary stays competitive → top performers stay → turnover costs drop → labor costs tied to output → EBITDA protected → predictable payroll → stable current ratio.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How do I convert hourly rate to annual salary?**
Standard formula: Hourly Rate × 2,080 = Annual Salary. Examples: $20/hr = $41,600/yr, $35/hr = $72,800/yr, $50/hr = $104,000/yr. For part-time (20 hrs/week): Hourly Rate × 1,040. Remember to add 25–35% for employer burden when comparing all-in costs.

**Q: What is a good employee turnover rate?**
Depends entirely on industry. Tech: below 13% is good. Healthcare: below 20% is good. Retail: below 50% is good. Hospitality: below 60% is good. Professional Services: below 12% is good. Any rate more than 5 points above your industry benchmark requires immediate root cause analysis.

**Q: What is a good EBITDA margin?**
SaaS: 20–35% is good. Professional Services: 20–30%. Manufacturing: 12–18%. Retail: 6–10%. Restaurants: 10–15%. Cross-industry rule: below 10% signals a structural cost problem needing urgent attention.

**Q: What current ratio do lenders require?**
Most commercial lenders require 1.5 minimum, with 2.0+ preferred. Below 1.0 means loan approval is almost certainly denied. The ideal zone is 1.5–2.5 — enough liquidity without holding idle capital.

**Q: How does employee turnover affect EBITDA margin?**
Directly and significantly. Every employee who leaves costs 50–100% of annual salary to replace. Those replacement costs compress EBITDA margin. Reducing turnover from 20% to 10% in a 100-person, $60K average salary business saves approximately $420,000 annually — adding 3.5 percentage points of EBITDA margin.

**Q: Can current ratio be too high?**
Yes. A ratio above 3.0–4.0 often signals excess idle cash, over-stocked inventory, or slow receivables collection. The ideal zone is 1.5–2.5. Above 2.5, consider whether the excess could be deployed more productively in the business.
