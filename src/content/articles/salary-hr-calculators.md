## Hourly to Salary Calculator: Convert Wages & Price Talent Correctly

**The Problem:** You're hiring a contractor at $35/hour and considering converting them to a salaried employee. What annual salary offer is fair? Or you're a freelancer wondering if your $28/hour rate is equivalent to your friend's $58,000 salaried job. Without the right conversion, you're either overpaying or losing talent to competitors who did the math.

### The Hourly to Annual Salary Formula:

```
Annual Salary = Hourly Rate × Hours Per Week × Weeks Per Year
```

**Standard full-time (US/Canada):**
```
Annual Salary = Hourly Rate × 40 hours × 52 weeks
Annual Salary = Hourly Rate × 2,080
```

### Simple Hourly to Salary Conversion Example:

**Scenario:** Your contractor charges $35/hour. What annual salary is equivalent?

```
Annual Salary = $35 × 2,080 = $72,800
```

But that's just the starting point. A true salary equivalent must account for benefits that hourly workers often don't receive:

**True Cost Comparison:**

| Component | Hourly Worker | Salaried Employee |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| Base compensation | $35/hr ($72,800/yr) | $72,800/yr |
| Health insurance | Not provided | $7,000–$15,000/yr |
| Paid time off (15 days) | Not paid | $4,200/yr |
| Employer payroll taxes | ~7.65% | ~7.65% |
| 401(k) match (3%) | Not provided | $2,184/yr |
| **True total cost** | **$78,373** | **$97,534** |

**Real Insight:** The contractor at $35/hour is actually cheaper than a $72,800 salaried employee when you factor in benefits. If you want true cost equivalency for a salaried hire, the hourly rate equivalent is closer to **$46.89/hour** — not $35.

### Salary to Hourly Conversion (Reverse):

If you know the annual salary and want the hourly equivalent:

```
Hourly Rate = Annual Salary ÷ 2,080
```

**Example:** A $85,000/year salaried employee costs:
```
$85,000 ÷ 2,080 = $40.87/hour
```

This is critical for project costing — if you bill a client $75/hour for a $40.87/hour employee, your gross labor margin is **45.5%**.

### Part-Time and Overtime Scenarios:

**Part-Time (20 hours/week):**
```
Annual = Hourly Rate × 20 × 52 = Hourly Rate × 1,040
```

**Overtime-Heavy Role (50 hours/week, 10hrs OT at 1.5x):**
```
Annual = (40 × Rate + 10 × 1.5 × Rate) × 52
Annual = (40 + 15) × Rate × 52 = Rate × 2,860
```

## Employee Turnover Rate Calculator: The Hidden Cost Destroying Your Margins

**The Most Expensive Line Item Nobody Tracks:** Employee turnover. Most businesses track revenue, COGS, and rent to the dollar — but have no idea what they spend annually on replacing people who leave. In 2026, the average US business spends **$42,000 replacing a mid-level employee** earning $60,000. That's 70% of annual salary, gone — in recruiting fees, lost productivity, training time, and onboarding costs.

### The Employee Turnover Rate Formula:

```
Turnover Rate (%) = (Employees Who Left ÷ Average Number of Employees) × 100
```

**Where:**
```
Average Employees = (Employees at Start of Period + Employees at End of Period) ÷ 2
```

### Employee Turnover Rate Calculation Example:

**Scenario:** Your company starts the year with 85 employees, ends with 90, and 12 employees left during the year.

```
Average Employees = (85 + 90) ÷ 2 = 87.5
Turnover Rate = (12 ÷ 87.5) × 100 = 13.7%
```

**What 13.7% means:** You replaced 13.7% of your workforce in one year. For context, the US national average is 12–15%. At 13.7%, you're average — but "average" still costs more than most business owners realize.

### Voluntary vs Involuntary Turnover:

**Voluntary Turnover** (employee chooses to leave):
- Indicates dissatisfaction, better offers, poor management
- Controllable — you can fix the causes
- More expensive because you lose institutional knowledge

**Involuntary Turnover** (company terminates):
- Includes performance-based separations and layoffs
- Partially controllable through better hiring and performance management
- Also costly — severance, legal risk, team morale impact

**Healthy ratio:** Voluntary turnover should be less than 60% of total turnover. If most of your departures are voluntary, your retention strategies need urgent attention.

## EBITDA Margin Calculator: Measure Your True Business Profitability

**The Problem with Net Profit:** Net profit is influenced by financing decisions, tax strategies, and accounting methods — making it nearly impossible to compare two businesses fairly. A company with heavy debt looks unprofitable on net income even if its core operations are healthy. A company with aggressive depreciation looks poor even with excellent cash generation.

**EBITDA strips all of that away** — giving you a clean view of operational profitability that is directly comparable across companies, industries, and time periods.

### The EBITDA Formula:

```
EBITDA = Net Income + Interest + Taxes + Depreciation + Amortization
```

Or calculated from revenue:

```
EBITDA = Revenue − Operating Expenses (excluding D&A, interest, taxes)
```

### The EBITDA Margin Formula:

```
EBITDA Margin (%) = (EBITDA ÷ Total Revenue) × 100
```

### EBITDA Margin Calculation Example:

**Scenario:** A professional services firm with the following financials:

| Line Item | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Total Revenue | $2,400,000 |
| Cost of Goods Sold | $960,000 |
| Gross Profit | $1,440,000 |
| Operating Expenses | $720,000 |
| EBIT (Operating Income) | $720,000 |
| Interest Expense | $85,000 |
| Taxes | $168,000 |
| Net Income | $467,000 |
| Depreciation & Amortization | $142,000 |

**EBITDA Calculation:**
```
EBITDA = $467,000 + $85,000 + $168,000 + $142,000 = $862,000
EBITDA Margin = ($862,000 ÷ $2,400,000) × 100 = 35.9%
```

**What 35.9% means:** For every dollar of revenue, the business generates $0.359 in operational cash profit before financing and accounting adjustments. This is an **excellent** EBITDA margin for a professional services firm.

### Why EBITDA Matters Beyond Internal Analysis:

**1. Business Valuation:**
Most business acquisitions are priced as a multiple of EBITDA:

| Business Type | Typical EBITDA Multiple |
|--------------|------------------------|
| Small business (<$1M EBITDA) | 3–5x |
| Mid-market ($1M–$10M EBITDA) | 5–8x |
| Large/established (>$10M) | 8–15x |
| High-growth SaaS | 15–25x |

**Example:** Your business has $500,000 EBITDA and an industry multiple of 5x.
```
Business Value = $500,000 × 5 = $2,500,000
```
Improve EBITDA by 20% (to $600,000): Business value becomes $3,000,000. **One operational improvement added $500,000 to your exit value.**

**2. Loan Qualification (DSCR):**
Banks use EBITDA to calculate Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR):
```
DSCR = EBITDA ÷ Annual Debt Service (Principal + Interest)
```
Most lenders require DSCR > 1.25. Below 1.0 means your EBITDA can't even cover debt payments — loan denied.

**3. Investor Screening:**
Private equity and angel investors screen deals by EBITDA margin before doing anything else. A business with <8% EBITDA margin is typically not investment-grade unless there's a clear operational fix.

### The EBITDA Improvement Framework:

**To increase EBITDA Margin, attack three levers:**

**Lever 1 — Revenue Growth (numerator up):**
- Raise prices (even 3–5% with no churn = direct EBITDA improvement)
- Improve sales conversion rate
- Add high-margin products/services
- Reduce discounting

**Lever 2 — Cost Reduction (denominator down):**
- Vendor renegotiation (target 5–10% reduction in top 5 suppliers)
- Automation of repetitive tasks (reduces labor cost without headcount cuts)
- Office space optimization (remote/hybrid reduces rent)
- Energy and utility audits

**Lever 3 — Mix Shift (sell more of what's most profitable):**
- Identify highest-margin product lines
- Focus sales incentives on high-margin SKUs
- Discontinue low-margin product lines dragging the average down

**Real Example — EBITDA Margin Improvement:**

| Metric | Before | After (12 months) | Change |
|--------|--------|-------------------|--------|
| Revenue | $1,200,000 | $1,320,000 | +10% |
| Operating Costs | $1,056,000 | $1,108,800 | +5% |
| EBITDA | $144,000 | $211,200 | +46.7% |
| EBITDA Margin | 12% | 16% | +4 pts |

**A 4-point EBITDA improvement on a business valued at 5x EBITDA adds $335,000 in business value** while generating $67,200 more in annual cash — from the same revenue base.

## Current Ratio Analysis Calculator: Evaluate Short-Term Financial Strength

**The Question Every Lender Asks First:** "Can this business pay its bills for the next 12 months without emergency financing?" Your Current Ratio answers it in a single number — and it determines whether you get the loan, the supplier credit terms, and the investor interest.

### The Current Ratio Formula:

```
Current Ratio = Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities
```

**Where:**
- **Current Assets:** Cash, accounts receivable, inventory, prepaid expenses — anything convertible to cash within 12 months
- **Current Liabilities:** Accounts payable, short-term debt, accrued expenses, current portion of long-term debt — anything due within 12 months

### Current Ratio Calculation Example:

**Scenario:** A manufacturing business preparing for a bank loan application:

**Current Assets:**
| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Cash & equivalents | $85,000 |
| Accounts receivable | $142,000 |
| Inventory | $98,000 |
| Prepaid expenses | $18,000 |
| **Total Current Assets** | **$343,000** |

**Current Liabilities:**
| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Accounts payable | $72,000 |
| Accrued expenses | $38,000 |
| Short-term loan | $45,000 |
| Current portion of long-term debt | $28,000 |
| **Total Current Liabilities** | **$183,000** |

**Current Ratio Calculation:**
```
Current Ratio = $343,000 ÷ $183,000 = 1.87
```

**What 1.87 means:** For every dollar of short-term obligations, this business has $1.87 in short-term assets. This is a **healthy ratio** — above the 1.5 lender minimum and approaching the 2.0 ideal.

### How to Interpret Your Current Ratio:

| Current Ratio | Status | What It Means | Lender View |
|--------------|--------|---------------|-------------|
| Below 1.0 | 🔴 Critical | Can't cover short-term obligations without new financing | Loan almost certainly denied |
| 1.0–1.2 | 🟠 Concerning | Very tight liquidity — one bad month could cause default | High scrutiny, higher rates |
| 1.2–1.5 | 🟡 Workable | Manageable but limited buffer | Loan possible with strong other metrics |

### Current Ratio vs Quick Ratio: Which to Use?

**Current Ratio** includes all current assets — including inventory.

**Quick Ratio** (Acid Test) excludes inventory — a stricter test:
```
Quick Ratio = (Current Assets − Inventory) ÷ Current Liabilities
```

**Using the same example:**
```
Quick Ratio = ($343,000 − $98,000) ÷ $183,000 = $245,000 ÷ $183,000 = 1.34
```

**When to use which:**

| Scenario | Use Current Ratio | Use Quick Ratio |
|----------|------------------|-----------------|
| General liquidity overview | ✅ | |
| Inventory-heavy business (retail, manufacturing) | | ✅ |
| Bank loan application | ✅ | ✅ (banks check both) |
| Investor due diligence | | ✅ |
| Service business (low/no inventory) | ✅ | Same result |

**Rule:** If your business holds significant inventory that could become hard to liquidate quickly (seasonal goods, specialized parts, perishables), your Quick Ratio is the more honest metric. Banks will check both.

### How to Improve Your Current Ratio Before a Loan Application:

**Strategy 1 — Accelerate Receivables Collection:**
- Send invoices immediately upon delivery
- Offer 2% early payment discount (2/10 net 30)
- Follow up on 30+ day outstanding invoices aggressively
- Consider invoice factoring for large outstanding amounts

**Impact:** Reducing average receivables collection from 45 days to 30 days on $142,000 AR frees approximately **$47,333 in cash** — moving it from receivable to cash (same current assets, but more liquid).

**Strategy 2 — Delay Payables Strategically:**
- Negotiate Net 45 or Net 60 terms with key suppliers
- Pay on the last day terms allow — not early
- Do NOT pay early unless you're capturing a significant discount

**Strategy 3 — Reduce Short-Term Debt:**
- Pay down revolving credit lines before application date
- Refinance short-term debt into long-term debt (moves liability out of current category)

**Impact Example:**
- Refinance $45,000 short-term loan into 3-year term loan
- Current liabilities drop from $183,000 to $138,000
- Current Ratio improves: $343,000 ÷ $138,000 = **2.49** (from 1.87)

**Strategy 4 — Optimize Inventory Levels:**
- Reduce slow-moving inventory (improves cash, reduces carrying costs)
- Implement just-in-time ordering where possible
- Conduct quarterly inventory audits to identify obsolete stock

## How All 4 Metrics Connect: The Business Health Dashboard

Here's what most business finance articles miss: these four metrics aren't independent — they're deeply interconnected. A problem in one almost always shows up in the others.

### The Business Health Chain:

```
Salary Decisions → Turnover Rate → EBITDA Margin → Current Ratio
```

**Here's how it works in practice:**

**Step 1 — Underpriced Salaries (Hourly to Salary):**
You hire at $18/hour ($37,440/year) when the market rate is $22/hour ($45,760/year).

**Step 2 — High Turnover (Employee Turnover Rate):**
Underpaid employees leave at 35% turnover rate vs industry average of 15%. You're replacing 20% more of your workforce than competitors.

**Step 3 — Compressed EBITDA (EBITDA Margin):**
Each replacement costs $25,000 in a 50-person company with 35% turnover = 17.5 replacements × $25,000 = **$437,500 in turnover costs annually** — directly hitting your operating expenses and crushing EBITDA margin.

**Step 4 — Weak Current Ratio:**
High operating costs from turnover drain cash. Accounts payable stretch as cash flow tightens. Current liabilities rise. Current ratio falls below 1.5. Bank denies the expansion loan you need to grow.

**The Fix Runs Backwards:**
Pay $22/hour (correct salary) → Turnover drops to 12% → Turnover costs drop by $250,000 → EBITDA margin rises 3–4 points → Cash position improves → Current ratio returns to 2.0+ → Loan approved.

**The paradox:** Paying employees more often costs the business less. The businesses that underpay to "save money" are often the ones spending the most on recruitment.

## 7 Common Mistakes That Distort Your HR & Financial Metrics

### 1. ❌ Using Gross Hourly Rate Without Benefits Loading

**The Mistake:** Comparing contractor rate ($40/hr) to employee salary ($83,200/yr = $40/hr) as if they're equal.

**The Fix:** Add 25–35% employer burden (benefits, payroll taxes, PTO) to any salaried employee cost before comparing to hourly/contractor rates.

**Impact:** Decisions made without benefits loading consistently underestimate true labor costs by $15,000–$30,000 per employee annually.

### 2. ❌ Counting Only Voluntary Turnover

**The Mistake:** Only tracking resignations, not including involuntary terminations (performance exits, layoffs) in turnover rate.

**The Fix:** Track **total separations** — voluntary + involuntary — to get a true workforce stability picture.

**Why it matters:** High involuntary turnover signals a hiring quality problem. High voluntary turnover signals a retention problem. You need to know which is which — and you can't without tracking both separately.

### 3. ❌ Using Net Income Instead of EBITDA for Business Comparison

**The Mistake:** Comparing your 8% net margin to a competitor's 12% net margin and assuming they're more profitable.

**The Fix:** Compare EBITDA margins. Your competitor might have lower debt interest (financing advantage) or different depreciation schedules (accounting difference) — neither of which reflects true operational performance.

**Tool:** Our **[EBITDA Margin Calculator](/calculator/ebitda-margin-calculator)** generates the operational comparison number that strips out financing and accounting noise.

### 4. ❌ Checking Current Ratio Only at Year-End

**The Mistake:** Looking at current ratio during annual accounting and ignoring it the other 11 months.

**The Fix:** Calculate monthly — or at minimum quarterly. Current ratio changes with every invoice paid, every loan drawdown, and every collection received. A ratio of 2.1 in January can become 0.9 in April if seasonal cash outflows aren't planned for.

### 5. ❌ Ignoring Industry-Specific Turnover Benchmarks

**The Mistake:** Panicking about 25% turnover in hospitality (below average) or feeling comfortable about 25% turnover in tech (dangerously high).

**The Fix:** Always compare turnover against **your industry benchmark** — not the national average. A turnover rate 5 points below your industry average is healthy regardless of the absolute number.

### 6. ❌ Treating EBITDA as Cash Flow

**The Mistake:** Business owners spend based on EBITDA thinking it equals available cash.

**The Fix:** EBITDA ≠ Free Cash Flow. EBITDA doesn't account for capital expenditures, working capital changes, or debt principal repayments. A business with 20% EBITDA margin can still run out of cash if capex is high and receivables are slow.

**True cash available ≈ EBITDA - Capex - Debt Principal - Working Capital Changes**

### 7. ❌ Optimizing Current Ratio at the Expense of Profitability

**The Mistake:** Holding excess cash to boost current ratio while that cash could be deployed in revenue-generating investments.

**The Fix:** A current ratio above 3.0 often signals under-investment. The goal is to be in the 1.5–2.5 range — enough liquidity to be safe, not so much that you're sitting on idle capital.

**The signal:** If your current ratio is 3.5+ but EBITDA margin is 8%, you have a profitability problem masked by cash hoarding. Redeploy the excess into the business.

## 5 Proven Strategies to Improve All 4 Metrics Simultaneously

### Strategy 1: Annual Compensation Benchmarking (Fix Salary → Fix Turnover → Fix EBITDA)

**The Action:** Every January, run every salaried position through the **[Hourly to Salary Calculator](/calculator/hourly-to-salary-calculator)** and compare against market data (LinkedIn Salary, BLS Occupational Outlook, Glassdoor).

**The Rule:** Any position more than 10% below market rate gets a salary adjustment plan within 90 days.

**The Math:** If a $55,000/year employee is 15% below market ($63,250), giving a $8,250 raise seems expensive. But if it prevents a $40,000 replacement cost, you've **saved $31,750 net** — plus retained institutional knowledge.

**Expected Outcomes:**
- Turnover rate: Typically drops 8–15 percentage points within 12 months
- EBITDA margin: Improves 2–4 points as replacement costs drop
- Current ratio: Improves 0.2–0.4 as operating cash stabilizes

### Strategy 2: Turnover Root Cause Analysis (Fix Retention → Fix Margins)

**The Action:** For every voluntary separation, conduct a structured exit interview covering compensation, management, growth opportunities, work environment, and work-life balance.

**Track:** Quarterly turnover by department, manager, and tenure band (0–6 months, 6–12 months, 1–3 years, 3+ years).

**High early turnover (0–6 months):** Hiring or onboarding problem.
**High mid-tenure turnover (1–3 years):** Career growth or compensation problem.
**High senior turnover (3+ years):** Management or culture problem.

**Each of these has a different fix — and the data tells you which to prioritize.**

**Expected EBITDA Impact:** Reducing turnover from 20% to 12% in a 100-person, $60,000 average salary business saves approximately **$336,000 annually** in replacement costs — directly adding to EBITDA.

### Strategy 3: Monthly EBITDA Review with Variance Analysis

**The Action:** Build a simple monthly P&L that calculates EBITDA margin automatically. Compare to prior month and prior year same month. Investigate any margin compression > 1.5 points.

**The Disciplines:**
- Revenue variance: Did sales miss? Did pricing change?
- Cost variance: Did any expense category spike?
- Mix variance: Did low-margin work dominate this month?

**Early warning system:** A business that checks EBITDA monthly catches margin erosion at -2 points. A business that checks annually finds out at -8 points — when it's much harder to reverse.

> **Track Monthly:** Use our **[EBITDA Margin Calculator](/calculator/ebitda-margin-calculator)** every month-end. Set a calendar reminder — it takes 5 minutes and gives you the single most important operational health signal available.

### Strategy 4: Cash Flow Management for Current Ratio Stability

**The 13-Week Cash Flow Forecast:**
Build a rolling 13-week cash flow projection. Each week, update actuals and reforecast the next 13 weeks. This gives you visibility 3 months out — enough time to act before a liquidity problem becomes a crisis.

**Key metrics to track weekly:**
- Days Sales Outstanding (DSO): Target < 35 days
- Days Payable Outstanding (DPO): Target 30–45 days (pay on terms, not early)
- Inventory Days: Target industry benchmark
- Cash conversion cycle: DSO + Inventory Days − DPO (lower is better)

**Current Ratio Goal:** Keep a minimum of 1.6 as your internal floor. If the ratio approaches 1.6, trigger a cash generation action plan before it hits 1.5.

### Strategy 5: Connect Compensation to Business Performance

**The Problem:** Salary increases are often given based on tenure, not performance or business results. This means you're paying for time served, not value delivered.

**The Fix:** Implement a structured compensation framework:

| Tier | Criteria | Salary Band | Increase Trigger |
|------|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Entry | Learning, supervised work | Market −10% to Market | Skill achievement milestones |
| Proficient | Independent, reliable | Market rate | Annual performance review |
| Senior | Mentors others, drives results | Market +10–15% | Business impact demonstrated |
| Expert | Critical/irreplaceable | Market +20–30% | Retention + performance |

**Why it improves all 4 metrics:**
- Salary stays competitive (correct ratio to market) ✅
- Top performers stay; poor performers self-select out (lower turnover cost) ✅
- Labor costs tied to output, not just headcount (EBITDA protection) ✅
- Predictable payroll reduces current liability surprises (stable current ratio) ✅

## Frequently Asked Questions About Salary & HR Calculators

### How do I convert hourly rate to annual salary?

**Standard formula:** Hourly Rate × 2,080 = Annual Salary (for 40 hours/week, 52 weeks)

**Examples:**
- $20/hour = $41,600/year
- $35/hour = $72,800/year
- $50/hour = $104,000/year

**For part-time (20 hrs/week):** Hourly Rate × 1,040

**For overtime-heavy roles (50 hrs/week):** Hourly Rate × 2,860

Remember to add 25–35% for employer burden (benefits, taxes, PTO) when comparing to an all-in contractor cost. Use our **[Hourly to Salary Calculator](/calculator/hourly-to-salary-calculator)** for instant, accurate results.

### What is a good employee turnover rate?

A "good" turnover rate depends entirely on your industry:

- **Technology:** Below 13% is good; below 10% is excellent
- **Healthcare:** Below 20% is good; below 15% is excellent
- **Retail:** Below 50% is good; below 35% is excellent
- **Hospitality:** Below 60% is good; below 45% is excellent
- **Professional Services:** Below 12% is good; below 8% is excellent

**Universal rule:** Any turnover rate more than 5 percentage points above your industry benchmark is a problem requiring immediate root cause analysis.

Calculate your exact rate with our **[Employee Turnover Rate Calculator](/calculator/employee-turnover-rate-calculator)** and compare against your industry benchmark.

### What is a good EBITDA margin?

EBITDA margin benchmarks vary significantly by industry:

- **SaaS/Software:** 20–35% is good; >35% is excellent
- **Professional Services:** 20–30% is good; >30% is excellent
- **Manufacturing:** 12–18% is good; >20% is excellent
- **Retail:** 6–10% is good; >12% is excellent
- **Restaurants:** 10–15% is good; >18% is excellent

**Cross-industry rule of thumb:**
- Below 10%: Structural cost problem — needs urgent attention
- 10–15%: Below average — improvement needed
- 15–20%: Healthy — room to optimize
- Above 20%: Strong — focus on maintaining and scaling

Calculate your EBITDA margin instantly with our **[EBITDA Margin Calculator](/calculator/ebitda-margin-calculator)**.

### What current ratio do lenders require?

Most commercial lenders have these thresholds:

| Lender Type | Minimum Current Ratio | Preferred |
|------------|----------------------|-----------|
| Traditional bank (SBA loan) | 1.2 | 1.5–2.0 |
| Commercial bank (business loan) | 1.5 | 2.0+ |
| Alternative lender | 1.0 | 1.3+ |
| Invoice/AR financing | 1.0 | N/A (AR-based) |
| Equipment financing | 1.0 | 1.2+ |

**Below 1.0:** Virtually impossible to secure traditional financing. Fix the ratio before applying.
**1.0–1.5:** Possible with strong revenue, collateral, or personal guarantee — but expect higher rates.
**1.5–2.0:** Standard approval range for most business loans.
**2.0+:** Strong approval with favorable rates and terms.

Check your ratio with our **[Current Ratio Analysis Calculator](/calculator/current-ratio-analysis)** before submitting any loan application.

### How does employee turnover affect EBITDA margin?

Directly and significantly. Every employee who leaves costs 50–100% of their annual salary to replace. Those replacement costs flow directly into operating expenses — compressing EBITDA margin.

**Example — 100-person company, $60K average salary:**

| Turnover Rate | Annual Replacements | Replacement Cost | EBITDA Impact |
|--------------|---------------------|-----------------|---------------|
| 10% | 10 employees | $420,000 | −3.5% margin |
| 20% | 20 employees | $840,000 | −7.0% margin |
| 30% | 30 employees | $1,260,000 | −10.5% margin |

*(Assumes $1.2M revenue base; replacement cost = 70% of salary)*

Reducing turnover from 20% to 10% saves $420,000 in this example — adding 3.5 points of EBITDA margin. That's often the difference between a business that gets funded and one that doesn't.

### Can current ratio be too high?

Yes. A current ratio above 3.0–4.0 often signals one of three problems:

1. **Excess idle cash** — money sitting in accounts earning nothing instead of being invested in growth
2. **Over-stocked inventory** — capital tied up in slow-moving stock, increasing carrying costs and obsolescence risk
3. **Slow receivables collection** — money owed to you that you're not collecting efficiently

**The ideal zone is 1.5–2.5** for most businesses. Above 2.5, ask whether the excess assets could be deployed more productively:
- Pay down long-term debt (reduces interest expense → improves EBITDA)
- Invest in growth initiatives (marketing, new hires, equipment)
- Return capital to owners
- Acquire a competitor or complementary business

High current ratio with low EBITDA margin is a common pattern in over-cautious businesses that hoard cash rather than investing in growth.
