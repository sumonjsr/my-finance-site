## What Is Sales Tax in the USA?

Sales tax is a percentage added to the price of a product or service at the point of sale. When you buy a $100 item in a state with 8% sales tax, you actually pay $108 at checkout. That extra $8 goes directly to the state government.

Unlike income tax — which is collected by the federal government — **sales tax in the USA is controlled entirely by individual states**. This is why rates vary so much from state to state. There is no single national sales tax in America.

In fact, it gets even more complicated. Many counties and cities add their own local tax on top of the state rate. So the total sales tax you pay is often:

> **Total Sales Tax = State Rate + County Rate + City Rate**

For example, in Los Angeles, California:
- State tax: 7.25%
- LA County tax: 1.00%
- City tax: 0.25%
- **Total: 8.5%**

## Why Does Sales Tax Matter So Much?

Whether you're a consumer or a business owner, sales tax has a real impact on your money.

**For shoppers**, it means the price on the tag is never the final price. A $500 laptop in Oregon (0% tax) costs exactly $500. That same laptop in Tennessee (9.55% combined rate) costs around $548. That's a $48 difference on one purchase.

**For small business owners and freelancers**, it's even more serious. If you sell products to customers in the United States, you may be legally required to collect and remit the correct sales tax. Getting it wrong — even accidentally — can result in penalties, back taxes, and audits.

Since a 2018 Supreme Court case called *South Dakota v. Wayfair*, online sellers now have to collect sales tax in states where they have a certain amount of sales — even if they have no physical presence there. This is called **economic nexus**, and it changed everything for e-commerce businesses.

## How to Calculate USA Sales Tax Manually

If you want to calculate sales tax by hand, the formula is simple:

> **Sales Tax Amount = Price × (Tax Rate ÷ 100)**
> **Total Price = Price + Sales Tax Amount**

### Example 1 — Basic Calculation

You're buying a $250 pair of sneakers in Florida (6% state sales tax, no local tax).

- Sales Tax = $250 × (6 ÷ 100) = $250 × 0.06 = **$15.00**
- Total Price = $250 + $15 = **$265.00**

### Example 2 — With Local Tax Added

You're buying a $1,200 laptop in Chicago, Illinois. Illinois state tax is 6.25%, and Chicago adds another 2.25% local tax. Combined rate: **8.5%**.

- Sales Tax = $1,200 × 0.085 = **$102.00**
- Total Price = $1,200 + $102 = **$1,302.00**

### Example 3 — Working Backwards (Tax-Inclusive Price)

What if you already paid $540 for something and want to know how much was tax? The tax rate was 8%.

- Original Price = $540 ÷ 1.08 = **$500.00**
- Tax Paid = $540 − $500 = **$40.00**

The manual method works fine for simple cases. ## Sales Tax on Common Product Types

One thing that surprises many people: **not everything is taxed the same way**. Most states have special rules for certain types of products.

### Groceries
Many states exempt basic groceries from sales tax entirely, or tax them at a reduced rate. For example:
- **Texas**: Groceries are exempt
- **Illinois**: Groceries taxed at 1% (reduced rate)
- **Mississippi**: Groceries taxed at full 7% rate

### Clothing
- **New York**: Clothing items under $110 are tax-exempt
- **Pennsylvania**: Most clothing is exempt
- **California**: All clothing is taxable

### Medications and Medical Products
Most states exempt prescription drugs. Over-the-counter medications vary by state. Medical devices (like wheelchairs) are often exempt too.

### Digital Products (Software, E-books, Streaming)
This is a rapidly evolving area. Some states now tax digital downloads, software subscriptions, and even streaming services. If you run a digital business, this is worth researching carefully for each state you sell in.

## Sales Tax for Online Sellers and E-Commerce Businesses

If you run an online store — on Shopify, Amazon, Etsy, or your own website — sales tax is something you cannot ignore in 2026.

After the Wayfair ruling, most states now require online sellers to collect sales tax if they cross certain thresholds. The most common threshold is:

- **$100,000 in sales** to customers in that state, OR
- **200 transactions** in that state

These are called **economic nexus thresholds**. Once you cross them, you're legally required to collect and remit tax in that state — even if you've never set foot there.

Here's what to do as an online seller:

1. **Track your sales by state** — know where your customers are
2. **Register for a sales tax permit** in states where you have nexus
3. **Collect the correct tax** at checkout (platforms like Shopify can automate this)
4. **File and remit** sales tax returns on time (monthly, quarterly, or annually depending on the state)

Missing any of these steps can lead to notices, penalties, and back-tax bills. ## Sales Tax Holidays — When You Can Shop Tax-Free

Here's a great tip for consumers: many US states hold **sales tax holidays** — usually a weekend each year — where certain items are completely tax-free.

Common sales tax holiday items include:
- **Back-to-school supplies** (clothing, notebooks, computers)

## Common Sales Tax Mistakes to Avoid

Whether you're a consumer or business owner, these are the most frequent sales tax errors:

**Mistake #1 — Assuming your state rate is the full rate**
Always check for county and city rates on top of your state rate. In many areas, local taxes add 1-3% on top.

**Mistake #2 — Using the wrong rate for digital vs physical products**
Some states tax physical goods but not digital goods, or vice versa. Don't assume the same rate applies to everything you sell.

**Mistake #3 — Ignoring economic nexus as an online seller**
If you're selling online and growing fast, check your sales by state regularly. Crossing the nexus threshold without knowing it is one of the most common and costly mistakes for e-commerce businesses.

**Mistake #4 — Not keeping records**
If you're a business, keep detailed records of all sales tax collected and remitted. If you're ever audited, this documentation protects you.

**Mistake #5 — Using outdated rates**
Sales tax rates change every year — sometimes multiple times per year. Always use an up-to-date calculator (like ours, which is updated regularly) rather than a table you found online years ago.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I have to pay sales tax when buying online?**
Yes, in most cases. Since the 2018 Wayfair ruling, online retailers are required to collect sales tax in states where they have economic nexus. Most major platforms (Amazon, eBay, Shopify) now handle this automatically.

**Q: Is sales tax the same as VAT?**
No. Sales tax is collected only at the final point of sale (when the consumer buys). VAT (Value Added Tax), used in the UK, Europe, and many other countries, is collected at every stage of the supply chain. The end result is similar for consumers, but the mechanics are different.

**Q: Can sales tax change during the year?**
Yes. State legislatures and local governments can change sales tax rates at any time. Rates often change at the start of a new fiscal year. This is why it's important to use an up-to-date calculator.

**Q: Do non-US residents pay US sales tax?**
Yes. If you're visiting the USA and buying something in a store, you pay sales tax just like a resident. Some tourists shop in tax-free states like Oregon specifically to avoid this.

**Q: What's the difference between sales tax and excise tax?**
Sales tax applies broadly to most retail goods. Excise tax is a special tax on specific products — like gasoline, alcohol, tobacco, or plane tickets. You often pay both on the same purchase.
